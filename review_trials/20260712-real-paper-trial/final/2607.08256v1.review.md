# Review: Best-of-N TTS Evaluation is Confounded by ASR Family Alignment

Paper ID: `2607.08256v1`  
Frozen PDF SHA-256: `98cb07ab642daada941cb6ddcc260290dfc181aec5ddc3fb29fc66438bc8b287`

## Summary

The paper studies a confound in Best-of-N zero-shot TTS evaluation: the apparent quality of an ASR verifier changes with the ASR evaluator used to compute WER. On a shared pool of F5-TTS candidates for 1,127 LibriSpeech-PC test-clean samples, it reports a verifier-order reversal between Whisper and wav2vec 2.0 evaluators, proposes two cross-family rank ensembles, and uses a small CKA pilot to explore whether encoder representation similarity explains evaluator agreement.

The narrow evaluator-dependence observation is useful, but several headline claims are broader than the displayed evidence.

## Strengths

1. The shared-candidate, uniform-normalization design isolates evaluator dependence more cleanly than comparisons across separate synthesis runs (Section 3 and Table 2, page 2).
2. The narrow verifier-order reversal is concrete: fwhisper-lgv3 prefers distil-v3, while w2v2-lv60 prefers w2v2-base on the displayed point estimates (Table 2 and Section 4.2, page 2).
3. Rank-average and max-rank are simple, training-free ensemble rules and attain competitive three-evaluator mean WER (Section 4.4, Figure 1, and Table 4, page 3).
4. The paper probes a mechanism with CKA rather than merely asserting one, and it leaves identity-versus-family coupling unresolved (Section 5, page 4).
5. The authors clearly disclose the single-backbone, single-corpus, and automatic-quality-metric scope (Section 6, page 4).

## Weaknesses

1. **The “same-family 2–3x” headline is contradicted by Table 3.** The displayed ratios are (26.0/7.9=3.29) for fwhisper-lgv3 and (26.1/18.2=1.43) for w2v2-lv60. HuBERT has no same-family verifier and gives a 27.1%–27.1% tie between the leading verifiers (Abstract, page 1; Table 3, page 3).
2. **The central interaction is not directly tested.** Table 2 gives point estimates, while Table 4 tests methods only against F5-TTS under fwhisper-lgv3. There is no direct within-evaluator verifier comparison or verifier-by-evaluator interaction test with multiplicity handling (pages 2–3).
3. **One promised evaluator is missing from the main results.** Section 3 announces four evaluators including distil-sm, but Tables 2–4 report only three. The omitted exact-identity condition is especially important for the identity-or-lineage explanation (pages 1–3).
4. **Reported p-values conflict.** For rows with the same displayed N=3 WER values, Table 1 reports p=0.31 for w2v2-base and p=0.030 for distil-v3, whereas Table 4 reports p=0.05 and p<0.001. The paper does not explain the difference (Tables 1 and 4, pages 2–3).
5. **The N=10 quality claim is not adequately evidenced.** The abstract connects “no measurable degradation” to the N=10 result, but Section 5.1 reports only N=3 automatic metrics, gives no per-configuration uncertainty, and notes possible UTMOS saturation (pages 1 and 4).
6. **The mechanism claim is too strong for the pilot.** Figure 2 uses only six evaluator-pair points; the reported correlation changes sign when one point is removed, and no uncertainty is shown. This supports “CKA is not clearly predictive here,” not a robust exclusion of representation similarity (page 4).

## Strongest falsification attempt

The broad family-alignment hypothesis predicts that each same-family verifier should recover materially and consistently more oracle headroom than a cross-family verifier. That prediction succeeds strongly only for fwhisper-lgv3 (3.29x), weakens to 1.43x for w2v2-lv60, and cannot be tested for HuBERT because there is no HuBERT verifier. The omitted full-data distil-sm evaluator also blocks the strongest exact-identity test.

This does not overturn the narrower finding that reported BoN rankings depend on the evaluator, but it does falsify the current general 2–3x formulation.

## Questions

1. Can the authors report paired confidence intervals or tests for every direct verifier comparison and a verifier-by-evaluator interaction test with multiplicity handling?
2. Where are the full 1,127-sample distil-sm evaluator results promised in Section 3?
3. Why do Table 1 and Table 4 give different p-values for rows with the same displayed N=3 WER values?
4. Were quality metrics computed for every N=5 and N=10 selection, and can the paper provide paired uncertainty or equivalence bounds and human or additional objective metrics?
5. Were the ensemble rules and N fixed before inspecting this test set, and do the results persist across candidate-generation seeds, another corpus, and another TTS backbone?

## Ethics and limitations

No direct ethics violation is established. However, lower aggregate WER on LibriSpeech-PC test-clean does not establish demographic or accent fairness, and a cross-family ensemble can still mask subgroup failures. The empirical scope is one TTS backbone, one English clean-speech subset, three fully reported evaluator columns, one shared candidate pool without seed robustness, automatic quality metrics near saturation, and no human listening study.

## Constructive comment

Narrow the abstract to evaluator-dependent ranking, disclose the fourth evaluator, reconcile the p-values, directly test verifier-by-evaluator interactions, report N=5/N=10 quality uncertainty, and present the CKA result as a pilot rather than a mechanism exclusion.
